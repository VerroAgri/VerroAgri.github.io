What are determinate, indeterminate, Annuals, Biennials and Perennials?

Posted on August 29, 2023 by Verro Agri Team
Farming Seed Breeding

What exactly is the difference between determinate and indeterminate? What is the difference between Annuals, Biennials and Perennials?

If any of the above questions spark your scientific curiosity, then you've absolutely come to the right place. But to answer the above questions, we must first frame such inquiries with the right context. In this case, we must first understand what the main scientific principles involved in plant reproduction.
Plants mainly reproduce through one of two means: sexually or asexually.

As for asexual reproduction, it is often seen as the result of modification of vegetative structures like stems or roots possessing sufficient fleshy tissue for energy storage. Alternatively, apomixes is another form of asexual reproduction and it entails two main types which result in the formation of seedlike structures: vivipary and agamospermy.
1- Vivipary: “often expressed by the conversion of the vegetative spikelet or leaf into a somatic structure with reproductive capability”
2- Agamospermy: “occurs when nucellar (unfertilized) tissue in the embryo sac develops into a diploid egg cell that further differentiates into a "seed."”

Pollination is necessary for agamospermy even if fertilization is rarely the case. The majority of apomixis-dominant plants also exhibit a small amount of sexual reproduction.

Since sexual fertilization does not occur during asexual reproduction, the majority of the offspring are guaranteed to be genetically similar. Under appropriate circumstances, asexually reproduced species thrive. The drawback of this reproductive strategy is that uniformity in the gene pool prevents children from being diverse enough to respond to unfavorable or unusual environmental circumstances. Furthermore, compared to seeds produced by sexual reproduction, tissues produced through vegetative reproduction other than apomixis result in storage, transport, and planting of tissues that are frequently big and challenging to handle.

Sexual Reproduction is marked first by flower formation which consists of a period of vegetative growth named the “juvenile stage” followed by a reproductive stage that kicks into action in response to stimuli. As a result, plants are often characterized according to their flowering response to environmental cues over time. There are generally four differing types of floral production:
• Annuals. Plants that flower and die in one season.
• Winter annuals. Plants that are established in late summer or fall and flower in the spring.
• Biennials. Plants that live for most or all of two seasons. They are characterized by vegetative growth during the first season and reproductive growth in the second season.
• Perennials. Plants that live, flower, and produce seed each year after they have passed the juvenile stage.
Within all four of these types, light and temperature have a marked effect in stimulating the flowering response

Plants can also be classified according to their flowering patterns:
1- Determinate: Which includes plants that produce all their flowers simultaneously during their season with little vegetative growth
2- Indeterminate: Which includes plants that produce their flowers intermittently with continuous vegetative growth

In general, it is important for farmers to know the classification, variety and flowering patterns of their crops to ensure that they align with their local natural conditions, professional expertise and the needs of their local market.

Reference:

McDonald, M. F., & Copeland, L. O. (2012). Seed production: principles and practices. Springer Science & Business Media.
